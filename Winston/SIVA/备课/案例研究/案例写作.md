Here are some tips for case writing:
1. Choose an interesting case. The case should be intriguing and meaningful. It helps if it illustrates important concepts or lessons. Real-world cases that describe actual events tend to be the most engaging.
2. Focus on key issues. Identify the central issues or problems in the case. Don't include irrelevant details. Frame the case around 2-5 key issues or questions.
3. Provide context. Give the background and context surrounding the case. Describe the environment, industry, organization, geographic location, time period, etc. The context helps readers understand why key issues exist.
4. Show multiple perspectives. Look at the issues from different angles. Discuss various stakeholder perspectives of challenges and possible solutions. A good case often provides a balanced analysis.
5. Be concise yet detailed. Get to the point but provide sufficient details about the issues. Be concise but make your descriptions vivid. Help readers experience the situation. Short cases (3 to 10 pages) tend to work best.
6. Raise unresolved questions. Conclude by highlighting key dilemmas, unanswered questions, and areas for further discussion. Open-ended cases encourage active participation. But don't leave major issues unaddressed.
7. Share teaching notes (optional). If teaching the case, provide notes on how to use the case most effectively. Discuss questions that provoke participation. Describe insights, solutions, and key takeaways you hope to convey. Notes help other teachers use and learn from your case.
8. Consider using visuals (optional). Images, diagrams, timelines, maps, and other graphics help bring a case to life. There are several key differences between a case study and a story:
1. Purpose. The purpose of a case study is primarily educational. It aims to teach readers something by presenting an in-depth analysis of a problem or situation. A story's purpose is more entertainment or creative expression. It seeks to inspire imagination or convey universal themes.
2. Focus. A case study focuses on analytical details and critical thinking. It looks at the who, what, where, how and why behind a situation. A story evokes emotion and empathy. It focuses more on connecting the reader to the characters and plot.
3. Events. In a case study, events are presented objectively as facts. They provide context and evidence to ground the analysis. In a story, events are subjectively dramatized through active characters and plot. They draw the reader in through conflicts and progression.
4. Structure. A case study follows a structured format with introduction, background, analysis, issues, and conclusions. A story has a narrative arch with exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution. Stories follow the classic storytelling structure.
5. Voice. Case studies use an objective, evidence-based voice. The author's voice takes a backseat to the facts. Stories often use creative prose with an active, subjective voice to shape the narrative and engage readers.
6. Outcome. The outcome of a case study is a logical solution or recommendation based on analysis. A story's outcome offers resolution or lessons learned. Stories are meant to satisfy through a denouement or conclusion.
7. Reader experience. Case studies challenge readers intellectually. Readers take on the role of problem-solver or decision-maker. Stories transport readers emotionally. Readers become immersed in the lives and challenges of characters.So in summary, the two forms differ substantially in their purpose, focus, structure, voice, and effect on the reader. But both remain powerful ways to discover and relate insights about the human experience. They simply operate at different levels.Does this help highlight the key differences between case studies and stories? Let me know if you have any other questions! (edited)

Here are some tips for teaching students to write research case studies:
1. Focus on research skills. 
   Explain that case writing requires investigating an issue in depth. Students need to gather data from sources like articles, interviews, reports, and documents. Discuss how to conduct searches, evaluate information, and synthesize research.
2. Choose an interesting issue. 
   Have students pick a problem or topic that interests them and could benefit from deeper analysis. Then help them frame a research question around the issue to guide their exploration. Real-world issues that have social relevance often work well.
3. Teach analysis and objectivity. 
   Discuss how to analyze research to identify key issues or themes. Help students consider perspectives from multiple sides. Explain that the voice should stay objective, letting the facts and analysis speak for themselves rather than advancing a biased viewpoint.
4. Use a standard structure. 
   Present the typical sections of a case study: introduction, background, research methodology, analysis, issues, conclusions, recommendations, references. Explain the purpose of each section. Providing a standard template can help students organize their work.
5. Have students share drafts. Require students to share initial case drafts with peers for reviews and feedback. Their classmates can check if the sections seem comprehensive, facts are accurate, analysis is sufficiently rigorous, and conclusions are well supported. Revision based on feedback improves quality.
6. Review strong examples. Analyze case studies that represent a high standard of research and analysis. Discuss elements that make them effective, such as how they establish significance, review literature, incorporate data, consider counterarguments, connect topics, and structure discussion. Strong examples help give students vision for what they can achieve.
7. Require high quality references. 
   Demand multiple references from credible sources to support key facts and analysis. Students should synthesize references rather than just listing quotes. Check that references are properly cited. Requiring strong referencing reinforces the research basis of case writing.
8. Provide individual guidance. 
   Meet with each student separately during drafting to review their work in progress and provide tailored guidance. Point out areas of strength and areas that could use improvement based on your standards for research quality and analytic depth. Individual support helps students develop expertise.
9. Consider publishing top cases. 
   Select a few standout case studies from your students' work that could have wider value and interest. Explore options for publication in a case journal, news outlet, website or educational repository. Publishing demonstrates impact and rewards excellence. Students can also cite their work as a publication for career purposes.


I hope this advice provides some helpful guidance for teaching your students to develop compelling research case studies! Let me know if you have any other questions. (edited)
